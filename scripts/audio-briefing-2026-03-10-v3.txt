Section Eleven. Google Bayesian Teaching.

Google Research may have cracked one of the deepest problems in AI: teaching models to actually learn from experience, not just pattern-match from training data.

The paper is called Bayesian Teaching, and the idea is elegant. Instead of fine-tuning an LLM on correct final answers, you train it to mimic the intermediate probabilistic predictions of an optimal Bayesian model. The result? An AI that maintains uncertainty, weighs new evidence, and continuously refines its beliefs — exactly how a statistician would reason.

The experiment was a flight recommendation task across five rounds. Off-the-shelf models like Gemma and Qwen plateaued after one round, defaulting to heuristics. But Bayesian-taught models improved with every interaction, agreeing with mathematically optimal predictions eighty percent of the time.

The real breakthrough: models trained exclusively on synthetic flight data transferred their reasoning ability to hotel recommendations and web shopping — domains they'd never seen. The probabilistic logic wasn't memorized. It was a transferable reasoning capability.

This points to the next generation of AI agents — ones that genuinely improve the more they work with you. Current agents are stateless. They help, then they forget. Bayesian Teaching changes that equation.

Section Ten. Deloitte's 2026 Global Human Capital Trends.

Eighty-five percent of leaders say building adaptability is critical — but only seven percent say they're actually leading it.

Deloitte introduces "culture debt" — the idea that every month you delay redesigning work around AI, you accumulate negative consequences that compound. Sixty-five percent of organizations say their culture must change significantly because of AI, but only six percent are making progress on human-AI work design.

The old change management playbook is dead. Deloitte calls the replacement "changefulness" — embedding continuous learning directly into how work gets done. For federal human capital leaders, this is the report to cite in every conversation.

Section Nine. McKinsey on Sovereign AI.

One hundred billion dollars in projected global sovereign AI investment for 2026. McKinsey maps the four-actor ecosystem: governments shape trust and rules, providers build the technology, enterprises convert it to value, and investors fund the transition.

Sovereignty isn't binary — it's a spectrum across data residency, compute location, model provenance, and operational control. Most enterprises have it on their roadmaps. Few have strategies, budgets, or workload tiering. For the federal government, this is existential.
