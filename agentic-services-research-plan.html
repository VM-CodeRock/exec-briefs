Agentic Services: Cost-Share Model for Federal Consulting

Research plan for a new Changeis service offering that replaces billable FTEs with AI agents, shares cost savings with clients, and increases margins — without reducing headcount on paper.

Research Plan · March 11, 2026 · 12 min read · 7 research streams
The Core Thesis

Federal agencies pay for labor hours, not outcomes. A typical T&M contract bills $150–250/hr per FTE for work that AI agents can increasingly perform — document processing, data analysis, report generation, compliance monitoring, system testing. What if Changeis deployed agents alongside its consultants, reduced the labor hours billed to the client, and split the savings?

The client gets the same (or better) outcomes at lower cost. Changeis keeps a percentage of every dollar saved — creating a margin structure that scales with automation, not headcount. This research plan outlines how to build, validate, price, contract, and sell that model.

I. The Economic Model

Why This Works: The Math Behind Agent-Augmented Delivery

The fundamental economics are compelling. Here's the simplified math on a single task order:

TRADITIONAL MODEL (Today) ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════ 10 FTEs × $180/hr avg loaded rate × 2,080 hrs/yr = $3,744,000 annual contract value Changeis margin: ~12-15% (typical T&M) Changeis profit: ~$450K-$560K AGENTIC MODEL (Proposed) ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════ 7 human FTEs + AI agents replacing 3 FTEs of effort Human labor billed: 7 × $180/hr × 2,080 = $2,620,800 Agent platform fee (to client): $280,000 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────── Client total cost: $2,900,800 Client savings: $843,200 (22.5% reduction) COST-SHARE SPLIT (60/40 client-favored) ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════ Client keeps: $505,920 (60% of savings) Changeis keeps: $337,280 (40% of savings) + Human margin: $393,120 (15% on 7 FTEs) + Agent costs: -$36,000 (API + infra) ──────────────────────────────────────────────────── Changeis profit: $694,400 Margin increase: +24% to +55% vs traditional

The key insight: Changeis's cost to run AI agents is a fraction of the cost to employ a human FTE. An AI agent capable of replacing junior analyst work might cost $3,000/month in API fees and infrastructure — versus $15,000+/month loaded cost for a human contractor. The spread between what the client was paying and what the agent actually costs is where the profit lives.

📅 Week 1, Days 1-3
Stream 2 — Federal Contract Mechanics

How to Structure Agent Delivery Within FAR/DFARS

  • T&M contracts — Can AI "labor" be billed as a separate CLIN? What rate structures work?
  • Firm Fixed Price (FFP) — Most natural fit for agent delivery (pay for outcomes, not hours). How to transition existing T&M?
  • Cost-plus — How do agent infrastructure costs flow through? Are they allowable?
  • Gain-sharing / shared savings — FAR provisions for incentive contracts (FAR 16.4). ESPC (Energy Savings Performance Contracts) as a precedent
  • CPAF / CPIF — Cost-Plus-Award-Fee or Cost-Plus-Incentive-Fee structures that reward efficiency
  • GSA Schedule implications — Can "AI agent services" go on a GSA schedule? What SIN codes?
  • Section 889 / CMMC / FedRAMP — Compliance requirements for AI platforms on government networks

Deliverable: Legal/regulatory analysis with recommended contract structures and a sample CLIN table.

Stream 3 — Pricing & Business Models

Five Models to Evaluate

Model How It Works Pros Risks
Gain-Share Client pays traditional rate, savings from agent use are split (e.g., 60/40) Easy to measure, aligned incentives Requires transparent baselining
Blended Rate Human + agent labor billed at a blended rate lower than pure-human rate Simple to implement, GSA-friendly Changeis absorbs agent costs
Outcome-Based Fixed fee per outcome (e.g., per report, per case processed) Maximum margin leverage Hard to define in government
Platform + Services Monthly platform fee for agent infrastructure + T&M for human oversight Recurring revenue, scalable Looks like a product, not a service
FTE Replacement Agent "seat" priced at 50-70% of equivalent human FTE rate Directly comparable savings Political sensitivity (replacing people)

Stream 5 — Case Studies & Precedents

Who Has Actually Done This in Government?

  • ESPC (Energy Savings Performance Contracts) — The strongest federal precedent for gain-sharing. DOE contracts where energy companies invest in upgrades and share savings with agencies over 10-25 years. Billions in contract value. Directly analogous model.
  • Share-in-Savings IT contracts — Authorized under the E-Government Act of 2002. Limited uptake but legally established. What went wrong? What can we learn?
  • VA's AI deployment — Claims processing automation. What contract structure did they use?
  • IRS Direct File — Cost savings from automated tax processing. How was it funded/measured?
  • USDA/NRCS — AI for conservation compliance. Small agency, big automation gains.
  • Private sector precedents — Wipro, TCS, Infosys gain-sharing IT outsourcing models. Directly transferable?

Deliverable: 5-8 case studies with contract structure, savings achieved, and lessons learned.

📅 Week 2, Days 1-3
Stream 6 — Risk & Ethics Framework

What Could Go Wrong (and How to Mitigate It)

  • Political risk — "Replacing government workers with AI" is toxic messaging. How to frame as "augmenting" not "replacing"?
  • Union considerations — AFGE, NFFE, other federal unions. Any CBA provisions about AI/automation?
  • Quality / liability — Who is liable when an agent makes an error on a federal deliverable? E&O insurance implications?
  • Data security — CUI, PII, classified data. Can agents touch it? Under what conditions?
  • Bias & fairness — AI in HR/workforce contexts especially sensitive. OMB M-24-10 requirements.
  • Measurement disputes — How to baseline and measure savings objectively? Third-party validation?
  • Technology risk — LLM costs are dropping but capabilities shift. How to handle model deprecation?
  • Client dependency — If the client decides to bring agent capabilities in-house, what's the off-ramp?

Deliverable: Risk register with likelihood/impact matrix and mitigation strategies for each.

VI. Key Hypotheses to Test

What We Believe — and What the Research Must Prove or Disprove

  • H1: AI agents can credibly replace 20-35% of billable labor on typical HC and IT consulting engagements within 12 months.
  • H2: A gain-sharing contract model is legally permissible under FAR and politically viable with current agency leadership.
  • H3: Federal CIOs and CORs will accept "agent hours" as a legitimate line item if coupled with human oversight and quality guarantees.
  • H4: The ESPC model (invest upfront, share savings over time) translates directly to AI-augmented consulting.
  • H5: Early movers in this space will establish contract precedents that create a durable competitive advantage.
  • H6: The biggest barrier is not technology but contract structure and agency risk tolerance.
  • H7: Internal change management at Changeis (consultant role evolution) is harder than external market adoption.

Decision Gate: After Phase 1, we'll assess whether the hypotheses hold up against market data. If H2 or H3 fail, we pivot the contract structure before investing in Phase 2.
