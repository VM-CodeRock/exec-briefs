Federal AI & Infrastructure Crisis

How AI is transforming — and disrupting — federal operations

Executive Summary

  • FAA Crisis Escalates: Government shutdown halts ATC training; staffing shortages force capacity reductions at major hubs including Detroit (219 delays), Houston (583 delays), and SFO (96 delays).

I.

FAA Staffing Crisis Forces Capacity Cuts

Infrastructure • Federal Operations

A partial government shutdown has halted air traffic controller hiring and training, forcing the FAA to reduce airport capacity at major hubs. The staffing crisis — persistent since 1981 despite multiple intervention attempts — reached a breaking point this week as hundreds of flights were delayed or canceled nationwide.

  • TSA staffing shortages: Security wait times affected at major airports including Charlotte Douglas (CLT)
  • Systemic strain: FAA acknowledged staffing pressures across multiple control centers managing airspace around Houston, Detroit, and Bay Area
  • Decades-old problem: SimpleFlying investigation reveals FAA staffing issues have persisted since 1981 despite various training method improvements
  • Spring break timing: Crisis coincides with peak travel season, compounding passenger disruption

Strategic Implication: The ATAPS-II contract ($103M, ongoing) directly addresses FAA technology modernization, but the staffing crisis demonstrates that technology alone cannot solve operational capacity constraints. Federal contractors positioned to accelerate ATC training pipelines or augment staffing with AI-assisted operations will be critical to near-term FAA recovery. This crisis validates workforce augmentation and training modernization as high-priority federal pain points.

II.

Google Deploys Agent Designer Across DoD

AI Infrastructure • Federal Adoption

Google Public Sector launched Agent Designer on GenAI.mil, a no-code platform enabling Department of Defense personnel to build custom AI agents using natural language. The tool gives 3+ million DoD employees the ability to automate multi-step workflows without programming knowledge, marking a major expansion of agentic AI in federal operations.

  • Use cases deployed: Action item summaries, award submission drafts, acquisition framework preparation, white paper research, leadership briefing materials
  • Service-wide adoption: Air Force/Space Force adopted GenAI.mil as enterprise platform (January 2026); Navy designated it as enterprise IT service for CUI and Impact Level 5 AI use
  • Platform momentum: Eight pre-built Gemini agents already live on GenAI.mil for immediate deployment
  • Timing advantage: Launch occurs amid Anthropic lawsuit against Pentagon, positioning Google as stable federal AI partner
"Agent Designer is a no- or low-code tool that lets users build AI agents using natural language. The capability enables all DOW personnel to create custom digital assistants to automate administrative processes and other multi-step tasks without programming knowledge." — Jim Kelly, VP Federal, Google Public Sector (Wash100 Award Winner)

Strategic Implication: Google's Agent Designer represents the federal government's first at-scale deployment of agentic AI tooling. The no-code approach democratizes AI capability creation across millions of federal employees — a direct play for the "long tail" of federal AI adoption. Competitors (Anthropic, Microsoft, OpenAI) must now match or exceed no-code agent builders to remain relevant in federal markets. For contractors: expect RFPs requiring agent-building capabilities and integration with GenAI.mil or equivalent platforms within 12-18 months.

III.

DOGE's ChatGPT Purge: 1,477 Grants Terminated

AI Governance • Policy Risk

Two junior DOGE staffers with no humanities background used ChatGPT to review and terminate 97% of active National Endowment for the Humanities grants in just 22 days. Newly released depositions reveal the pair fed project summaries into ChatGPT with a simple prompt: "Does the following relate at all to D.E.I.? Respond factually in less than 120 characters. Begin with 'yes' or 'no.'"

  • Staff background: Justin Fox and Nate Cavanaugh, both without academic experience in the humanities, assigned to review NEH portfolio
  • Methodology: Scraped short project summaries from internet, fed to ChatGPT with 120-character prompt asking if project related to "DEI"
  • Example ruling: Documentary on 1873 Colfax, Louisiana massacre of Black men by KKK flagged as "DEI" because "it focuses on exclusively anti-Black violence, which is a race"
  • Charles Ives casualty: Music Unwound, a multi-year NEH-funded consortium celebrating composer Charles Ives's sesquicentennial, terminated for mentioning Civil War and Black American musical contributions
  • Deposition struggles: When asked to define DEI in court deposition, Fox could not articulate a clear definition
  • Agency impact: Cavanaugh embraced DOGE's mission of shrinking "useless small agencies" — NEH nearly eliminated
"Fox and Cavanaugh functioned as hitmen in a self-fulfilling exercise in naïve casuistry. Nearly every active grant made during the Biden administration, they decided, was ideologically toxic." — Joseph Horowitz, Music Unwound Director (via ArtsJournal)

Strategic Implication: This case exposes catastrophic risks of deploying AI without domain expertise or governance guardrails. The use of ChatGPT as a decision engine — not decision support — for high-stakes policy actions sets a dangerous precedent. Federal agencies must immediately establish AI governance frameworks that mandate: (1) human domain expertise in the loop, (2) clear decision criteria beyond binary prompts, (3) appeal/review processes, and (4) accountability for AI-driven outcomes. For contractors building federal AI: expect new compliance requirements around explainability, human oversight, and audit trails within 6-12 months.

IV.

DoD Solicits Contractor Input on DFARS Overhaul

GovCon • Procurement Reform

The Department of Defense is asking defense contractors to recommend substantive changes to the Federal Acquisition Regulations and the Defense Federal Acquisition Regulation Supplement (DFARS). The request signals potential major reforms to federal procurement rules that could reshape how billions in defense contracts are awarded and managed.

  • Scope: DoD seeking contractor feedback on both FAR and DFARS frameworks — rare opportunity for industry to shape procurement policy
  • Timing context: Comes amid broader federal efficiency push and DOGE-driven agency restructuring
  • Potential impacts: Changes could affect contract award processes, compliance requirements, small business set-asides, and acquisition timelines
  • Industry opportunity: First major solicitation for procurement reform input in over a decade

Strategic Implication: DoD's request for contractor input suggests recognition that current procurement frameworks may hinder innovation and efficiency. Prime contractors should prioritize responses focused on: (1) reducing compliance burden without sacrificing oversight, (2) accelerating approval timelines for emerging tech (AI, quantum, cyber), (3) streamlining small business teaming arrangements, and (4) modernizing cybersecurity requirements (CMMC, etc.). Expect follow-on RFIs and proposed DFARS rule changes within 90-120 days. Companies that engage early will shape the rules they must follow.
