XI. AI Research

Google Bayesian Teaching: AI That Learns to Update Its Own Beliefs

Google Research published a paper — "Bayesian Teaching Enables Probabilistic Reasoning in Large Language Models" — that may represent one of the most significant shifts in how AI models are trained. Instead of teaching LLMs to memorize correct answers, researchers taught them to reason like Bayesian statisticians: maintaining uncertainty, weighing new evidence, and continuously refining their internal beliefs.

The core problem: current LLMs are static pattern matchers. They don't update their understanding as new information arrives during a conversation. They default to heuristics — always recommending the cheapest flight, always assuming the same preferences — rather than adapting to what they're learning in real time.

80% Agreement with optimal Bayesian predictions
Zero-shot Generalization to unseen domains
5 rounds Interactions to converge on preferences
  • The method: Bayesian Teaching — Instead of fine-tuning on the "correct" final answer (Oracle Teaching), researchers fine-tuned LLMs to mimic the intermediate probabilistic predictions of an optimal Bayesian model. The LLM learns to maintain a probability distribution over possible user preferences and update it with each interaction.
  • The experiment — An LLM acted as a flight recommendation assistant across 5 rounds. In each round, it presented 3 options (varying cost, duration, stops). The simulated user chose based on hidden preferences. Off-the-shelf LLMs (Gemma, Qwen) plateaued after one round. Bayesian-taught models continuously improved across all 5 rounds.
  • Bayesian beats Oracle — Counterintuitively, teaching the model to reason under uncertainty outperformed teaching it the "right" answers. The Bayesian-taught model agreed with mathematically optimal predictions 80% of the time. The Oracle-taught model didn't generalize — it memorized, it didn't learn to reason.
  • Zero-shot domain transfer — Models trained exclusively on synthetic flight data successfully transferred their Bayesian reasoning to hotel recommendations and real-world web shopping — domains they'd never seen. The probabilistic logic wasn't task-specific. It was a transferable reasoning capability.
  • Bridging symbolic AI and neural networks — Experts call this a paradigm shift: distilling rigid mathematical logic (Bayesian inference) into the flexible architecture of a neural network. LLMs don't need perfect training data — they can learn the process of reasoning from examples of uncertainty.
"By showing that LLMs can internalize core scientific principles of reasoning and generalize them to completely different industries, experts believe this method will accelerate the evolution of LLMs from static text-generators into highly adaptive, self-updating intelligent agents." — EvoAI Labs, research analysis

Researchers: Linlu Qiu (MIT), Fei Sha (Google Research), Kelsey Allen (Google DeepMind), Yoon Kim (MIT), Tal Linzen (Google Research), Sjoerd van Steenkiste (Google Research). Paper: arXiv:2503.17523.

Strategic Implication: This research points to the next generation of AI agents — ones that genuinely learn from interactions rather than resetting to zero with each conversation. For enterprise AI deployment, this means agents that improve their recommendations the more they work with a specific team, project, or client. For federal agencies: imagine a procurement assistant that learns agency-specific buying patterns over time, or a case management agent that refines its understanding of regulatory nuances with each decision. The current generation of AI "assistants" are stateless — they help, but they don't learn. Bayesian Teaching changes that equation. Watch for this capability to appear in commercial models within 12-18 months.

X. Workforce

Deloitte 2026 Human Capital Trends: The Adaptability Crisis

Deloitte's annual Global Human Capital Trends report — subtitled "From tensions to tipping points: Choosing the human advantage" — arrives at a moment when organizations face compounding pressure: relentless change, rising trust concerns around AI, and cultural friction, precisely when they need speed, resilience, and reinvention.

The core finding: the gap between what leaders say matters and what they're actually doing has become a chasm.

85% Say adaptability is critical
7% Say they're leading it
6% Making progress on human-AI work design
  • Change exhaustion is real — One-third of workers experienced 15 major changes last year. Only 27% of leaders say their organizations manage change well. The old "manage the change" approach is failing; Deloitte proposes shifting to "changefulness" — embedding continuous learning and in-the-moment support into the work itself.
  • AI is creating culture debt — 65% of organizations say culture must change significantly because of AI. 34% say culture is actively inhibiting AI transformation. 42% of workers say their organizations aren't evaluating AI's impact on people. The risk: optimizing for efficiency while accumulating "culture debt" — negative consequences from neglecting norms, ethics, and human connection.
  • Human-AI work design barely exists — 60% of executives use AI in decision-making, but only 5% say they manage it well. 56% design AI solely for business outcomes; only 40% design for both business and human outcomes. The report argues the real transformation isn't "adding humans and machines together" — it's redesigning work with clear decision rights and trust thresholds.
  • Traditional functions can't keep pace — 66% of C-suite leaders say traditional functions (HR, IT, finance, legal) must change, yet only 7% are making progress. 7 in 10 say their competitive strategy requires speed and agility — but functional silos prevent it.
"Organizations are facing a new reality. Change is relentless and the old playbook can't keep up. Leaders need to build adaptability into how work gets done so that their people have clarity, trust and the support to evolve with AI." — Simona Spelman, U.S. Human Capital Leader, Deloitte

The 85% vs. 7% gap — leaders who know adaptability matters but can't execute — is exactly where human capital consulting creates value. IX. Federal Strategy

McKinsey: Sovereign AI — From Ambition to Ecosystem

McKinsey's new report on sovereign AI moves the conversation from "should we?" to "how do we actually build it?" — and the answer is: not alone. Building sovereign AI capability requires coordinated action across four distinct actor groups, each with specific roles that can't be substituted.

$100B Projected global sovereign AI investment (2026)
4 actors Governments, providers, enterprises, investors
Most Have roadmaps, few have strategies
  • The four-actor ecosystem — Governments shape trust, rules, and demand. Providers create underlying technology and platforms. Enterprises convert infrastructure into real economic value. Investors fund the transition. The report argues all four must be linked across energy, compute, data, models, platforms, and applications.
  • Sovereignty is a spectrum — Not binary. Four dimensions define where you sit: data residency, compute location, model provenance, and operational control. Most organizations need different tiers for different workloads — sensitive defense work requires full sovereignty; routine analytics can tolerate less.
  • Enterprise interest is widespread, execution isn't — Most enterprises have sovereign AI on their 2026 roadmaps, but few have detailed strategies, action plans, budgets, or workload tiering. The gap between intent and implementation is the main risk.
  • Key risks — Mis-sequencing (building infrastructure ahead of demand), perceived higher costs of sovereign solutions, and fragmented partner ecosystems that make implementation difficult. Many sovereign AI initiatives are stalling and failing to deliver.
"Leaders are realizing that whoever controls the AI stack ultimately controls the outcomes. Agentic AI has turned sovereignty from an abstract concept into an operational one." — McKinsey, Agentic AI Governance

Strategic Implication: The federal government is, by definition, the sovereign AI use case. Data sovereignty, domestic compute, controlled model deployment, FedRAMP compliance — these aren't future concerns, they're current mandates. For Changeis, this report validates a positioning opportunity: federal agencies need partners who understand both the technology stack and the governance framework. The "workload tiering" concept is particularly actionable — helping agencies classify which AI workloads require full sovereignty versus which can use commercial cloud creates immediate consulting value. This maps directly to the FAA's ATAPS II modernization work.

VII. Federal Policy

SBIR/STTR Programs Revived — Five-Year Reauthorization with Teeth

On March 3, 2026, the Senate unanimously passed the Small Business Innovation and Economic Security Act, reauthorizing the SBIR and STTR programs for five years through September 30, 2031 — ending a five-month lapse that had frozen federal small business R&D funding since October 2025.

The legislation, a Markey-Ernst compromise, avoids the most aggressive reform proposals but implements meaningful changes across four areas:

5 years Reauthorization through 2031
$30M New Phase II "Strategic Breakthrough" cap
5 months Duration of program lapse
  • Strategic Breakthrough Allocation — New Phase II awards up to $30M over 4 years (vs. the current $1.5M/2-year cap). Limited to agencies with $100M+ in SBIR expenditures. Requires 1:1 matching funds from outside SBIR. For DoD, 20% of matching must come from new DoD funding. Designed to bridge the "valley of death" — but will reduce total award count.
  • Foreign ownership scrutiny expanded — Agencies must now analyze foreign affiliations of key personnel, investment relationships with "countries of concern," technology licensing agreements, and joint ventures with foreign entities. Coordination with intelligence community required. Rejected applicants get notice to make corrective actions.
  • SBIR mill caps — Congress punted the prescriptive approach: instead of setting universal limits, each agency must set its own cap on proposals per company per year/solicitation/topic. Watch for divergent agency interpretations — this will get messy.
  • Technical & Business Assistance (TABA) reform — Awardees can now choose their own third-party advisors (previously agency-assigned). Phase I: $6,500/project. Phase II: up to $50,000/project. New eligible uses include cybersecurity and foreign involvement screening.
"The Act reflects a compromise that stops well short of the more aggressive reforms that were in consideration. Still, it implements meaningful changes to the SBIR/STTR programs, including security and program oversight measures and a new higher-value option for Phase II awards." — Morrison & Foerster, Government Contracts Insights

Strategic Implication: The $30M Strategic Breakthrough awards create a new tier of SBIR opportunity for mature small businesses — but the matching fund requirement and DoD-specific conditions narrow eligibility significantly. For federal contractors and consultancies supporting small business clients, the expanded foreign-ownership due diligence and per-agency proposal caps mean compliance processes need updating now, before the House passes its version. The TABA reforms also open advisory revenue opportunities for firms that can help Phase II awardees with cybersecurity and foreign risk screening. House passage expected within weeks.

I. Open Source

Karpathy's AutoResearch: The Autonomous ML Research Loop

Andrej Karpathy — former Tesla AI lead, OpenAI co-founder, and coiner of "vibe coding" — released autoresearch, a minimalist 630-line Python script that automates the scientific method for machine learning. The agent reads its own source code, forms hypotheses, modifies training parameters, runs experiments within a fixed compute budget (typically 5 minutes on a GPU), evaluates results, and iterates — all without human involvement.

126 Experiments overnight
~700 Changes in 2 days
11% Efficiency gain
8.6M+ Views on X

In one overnight run, the agent drove validation loss from 0.9979 to 0.9697. Over two days of continuous operation, it processed approximately 700 autonomous changes, finding roughly 20 additive improvements that transferred to larger models — cutting the "Time to GPT-2" leaderboard metric from 2.02 hours to 1.80 hours. Karpathy noted the agent caught attention scaling and regularization oversights he had missed in two decades of manual work.

"Seeing the agent do this entire workflow end-to-end and all by itself… is wild." — Andrej Karpathy

The reaction was swift. Varun Mathur, CEO of Hyperspace AI, distributed the loop across a peer-to-peer network. On the night of March 8–9, 35 autonomous agents ran 333 experiments completely unsupervised:

  • Hardware diversity as strategy — H100 GPUs used brute force while CPU-only agents got creative, focusing on initialization and normalization strategies they could test within tighter compute budgets
  • Gossip-based discovery — When one agent found Kaiming initialization dropped loss by 21%, the discovery spread network-wide within hours via GossipSub protocol; 23 agents incorporated it into their own hypotheses
  • Compressed history — In 17 hours, agents independently rediscovered ML milestones like RMSNorm and tied embeddings — breakthroughs that took human researchers at Google Brain and OpenAI roughly eight years to formalize

Strategic Implication: This demonstrates AI-driven research loops applicable far beyond ML. Any domain involving hypothesis → experiment → evaluate cycles — marketing optimization, drug discovery, materials science — could benefit from this paradigm. The open-source MIT license makes enterprise adoption frictionless.

Microsoft's "Frontier Transformation" event on March 9 wasn't just a product launch — it was a platform declaration. Wave 3 of Microsoft 365 Copilot moves from "AI assistant" to "AI that does work," with embedded agentic capabilities across Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and Outlook. The event featured Jared Spataro (CMO, AI at Work), Charles Lamanna, and Sumit Chauhan.

The headline: Microsoft is no longer betting on a single AI model. It's building the platform where all models compete to serve enterprise work.

160% Copilot seat growth YoY
10× Daily active usage increase
500K Agents running inside Microsoft
90% Fortune 500 on Copilot
  • Copilot Cowork (built with Anthropic) — The biggest announcement. Built on the same agentic harness as Anthropic's Claude Cowork, but running in the cloud within a customer's M365 tenant — not locally on a device. Handles long-running, multi-step work: preparing a customer meeting by assembling a presentation, pulling financials, emailing the team, and scheduling prep — from a single request. Tasks run for minutes or hours, coordinating actions across apps. Spataro on Anthropic's local approach: "We actually don't work locally, and that's a feature, not a bug." Research preview in March via the Frontier program.
  • Claude in mainline Copilot — Anthropic's Claude is now available across the full Copilot Chat experience, not just the Researcher and Excel features where it was previously siloed. Microsoft calls this "multi-model intelligence" — Copilot automatically selects the right model for the task. "Every 60 days at least, there's a new king of the hill," said Spataro. "There's so much demand for a platform that doesn't feel like, 'I have to skip over to the next vendor.'"
  • Wave 3 Agentic Copilot in apps — Copilot in Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and Outlook now creates, edits, and refines content end-to-end inside the document. Uses "Work IQ" — Microsoft's intelligence layer that understands how you work, who you collaborate with, and what content matters. Previously called "Agent Mode" in preview, now it's just core Copilot. GA in Excel and Word; PowerPoint and Outlook rolling out over coming months.
  • Agent 365 — The control plane ($15/user, GA May 1) — Governs AI agents the same way IT manages employees: using Entra, Defender, Purview, and Intune. In two months of preview, tens of millions of agents appeared in the registry. Microsoft internally has 500K+ agents generating 65,000+ responses per day. IDC projects 1.3 billion agents in circulation by 2028. Spataro: "AI agents are as subject to phishing attacks as people are. As soon as an AI agent has an email address, they get spam too, and they can respond to it."
  • M365 E7: The Frontier Suite ($99/user, GA May 1) — Bundles E5 ($60) + Copilot ($30) + Agent 365 ($15) + Entra Suite ($12) = $117 à la carte, now $99 bundled. 13.2% discount. Gartner calls the value "questionable" for most knowledge workers today and warns against nonreduction contract clauses. But Microsoft is betting the bundle locks in enterprises before competitors offer alternatives.
  • MCP and open standards — Agents in Copilot Chat now support Apps SDK and MCP (Model Context Protocol) Apps, letting third-party tools surface directly in chat. Partners: Adobe, Monday.com, Figma, Dynamics 365.
"We really believe right now is an inflection point. The inflection point for us is Copilot taking on these agentic capabilities and going from assistance to real doing." — Jared Spataro, CMO for AI at Work, Microsoft (Fortune interview)
"I think I have the most data points of anyone in the industry; customers want per user right now. Doesn't mean they always will, but that's what they want currently." — Jared Spataro on why Microsoft is sticking with per-seat pricing over consumption

Named customers: NASA, U.S. Department of the Interior, Mercedes-Benz, Fiserv, ING, University of Kentucky, University of Manchester, Westpac.

III. Developer Tools

Anthropic Code Review: Multi-Agent Bug Detection for Claude Code

Anthropic launched Code Review, a multi-agent system integrated into Claude Code that automatically analyzes pull requests for logic bugs before human reviewers see the code. Available in research preview for Claude for Teams and Claude for Enterprise customers.

3× Review feedback gain
$2.5B+ Claude Code run-rate
4× Enterprise subs YTD

The problem is straightforward: Claude Code has dramatically increased code output, creating a bottleneck at the pull request review stage. As Cat Wu, Anthropic's Head of Product, put it: "Now that Claude Code is putting up a bunch of pull requests, how do I make sure those get reviewed in an efficient manner?"

The system integrates with GitHub and auto-triggers on PR creation. Multiple agents analyze the codebase in parallel from different perspectives — one might focus on control flow, another on data handling. A final aggregation agent deduplicates, ranks, and prioritizes findings. The focus is strictly on logic errors over style, with step-by-step reasoning that explains what each issue is, why it's problematic, and how to fix it.

Issues are classified by severity:

Critical — must fix Potential — worth reviewing Historical — preexisting issues

Engineering leads can customize additional checks based on internal best practices. Target customers include Uber, Salesforce, and Accenture — companies already using Claude Code at scale. Anthropic is also navigating a legal dispute with the Department of Defense over a supply chain risk designation, making its enterprise revenue line increasingly important.

Strategic Implication: As AI-generated code volume explodes, automated quality assurance becomes critical infrastructure. Anthropic is building the full pipeline — code generation (Claude Code) → review (Code Review) → security analysis (Claude Code Security) — creating significant switching costs for enterprise customers already in the ecosystem.

V. Strategy

AI for Boring Businesses: Four Lessons from Main Street

Sam Gerstenzang, former Stripe product leader and partner at incubator Boulton and Watt, argues that the real AI opportunity isn't in building the next SaaS darling — it's in operationally complex, real-world service businesses. His portfolio includes Meadow (a funeral home that uses wedding venues during the day) and Moxie (a platform for nurses starting aesthetic medicine clinics, which just raised a $25M Series C). The thesis: as software gets easy for anyone to build and public SaaS companies are down 70%+, resilient businesses with operational moats will win.

Four hard-won lessons from bringing AI to Main Street:

  • Research compressed from 3 months to 3 weeks — AI at every step: ChatGPT for market sizing and margin analysis, Clay for lead enrichment and outbound, Claude Code to spin up landing pages in an hour, Granola + Claude to extract insights from customer calls. Each pivot costs less, moves faster.
  • Reward outcomes, not AI usage — "AI initiatives" fell flat when they became checkbox activities. The danger: team members outsourcing judgment to "the hallucinations of a madman." The breakthrough came when an analyst used Claude Code to build an entirely new interactive map for customer density data — not just cleaning spreadsheets. Concrete examples of transformation beat mandates every time.
  • AI customer acquisition is just another channel — Generative Engine Optimization is the new SEO, and AI-generated email outreach is flooding inboxes. But OpenAI started selling ads on ChatGPT, and Google search revenue is still up 17% YoY. Treat AI channels like any new channel: test, measure, don't assume paradigm shift.
  • Humans remain stubbornly hard to replace — In operationally complex businesses (funeral homes, medical clinics), the human element isn't a weakness to optimize away — it's the value proposition. AI augments the humans; it doesn't replace the relationships, judgment, and regulatory navigation that these businesses run on.
"The use of AI itself is not the goal — using AI only matters if it can improve the quality and the speed of the work. Hallucinations are your problem, not the LLM's." — Sam Gerstenzang, Boulton and Watt

Strategic Implication: This is the Changeis playbook in a nutshell. Federal consulting is the definition of an "operationally complex, regulated, real-world service business." The firms that integrate AI into how they deliver — not just what they deliver — will outpace competitors who treat AI as a bolt-on. The lesson on rewarding outcomes over AI usage is directly applicable to any federal team rolling out AI tools: mandate results, not methods.

VI. Big Ideas

Analogue Intelligence: What AI Can't Replicate — and Why It Matters

Professor Karim Lakhani (Harvard Business School) introduces a concept he calls analogue intelligence — the accumulated output of human judgment, taste, craft, and sensibility that cannot be reduced to a dataset or reproduced by a system trained on one. It lives in a watchmaker setting a lever escapement under a loupe, a jazz musician deciding when not to play, a collector who knows a dial is right before consulting a reference.

Lakhani uses serious watch collecting as his lens, but the argument cuts much deeper: the real anxiety of the AI moment isn't that machines will replace human labor — it's that we might lose the conditions under which analogue intelligence is developed, transmitted, and valued at all.

The piece builds on Lakhani's foundational research into open-source software communities, where he and collaborator Bob Wolf surveyed 684 developers across 287 projects. Their finding: the strongest predictor of contribution wasn't career advancement or reputation. It was creative flow — the Csikszentmihalyi-an experience of skill meeting challenge. The second was obligation to community.

Lakhani maps this same motivational architecture onto watch collecting — and by extension, any domain of deep human expertise:

  • Intrinsic motivations — Three distinct types: the hunt (episodic, dopaminergic problem-solving), curation (architectural coherence across a collection), and aesthetic/engineering appreciation (contemplative engagement with craft)
  • Prosocial behavior — Knowledge commons maintained for free, custodial stewardship of significant pieces for future generations, and patronage of independent craftspeople as advocacy against industrial homogenization
  • Counter-signaling — Choosing the obscure and under-recognized as a signal to those capable of reading it that you're beyond needing to signal at all
  • Identity as self-portrait — The collection isn't consumption — it's a curated expression of who you are and what you value
"Analogue intelligence is not nostalgia. It is not anti-technology. It is a specific claim about the nature of certain kinds of knowledge — that they are embodied, contextual, and irreducibly personal in ways that make them both extraordinarily valuable and extraordinarily fragile." — Prof. Karim Lakhani, Harvard Business School

Strategic Implication: As AI automates analytical and generative tasks, the premium on analogue intelligence — human judgment, taste, contextual understanding, relationship navigation — increases, not decreases. For federal transformation leaders, this reframes the AI conversation: the goal isn't to replace human expertise but to free it from mechanical work so it can focus on the irreducibly human elements that drive mission outcomes. Organizations that understand this distinction will build better human-AI teams than those chasing full automation.
